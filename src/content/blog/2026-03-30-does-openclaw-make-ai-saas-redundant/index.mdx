I'm giving a talk at OpenClaw Manchester this week, and the title is deliberately provocative. But it's a question worth sitting with. The pitch for OpenClaw is compelling: open protocol, model-agnostic, connect to anything with an API. No monthly fee, no vendor lock-in. If that's all it takes, what is AI SaaS actually selling?

I've been thinking about this a lot, and I don't think the answer is "nothing". But I do think the answer has changed.

## Trust

The technical barrier to connecting AI to services is basically gone. But technical access was never really the hard part. The hard part is trust.

Consider Google Ads. We're building [AdPeak](https://adpeak.ai) at the moment, an AI tool for agencies managing client ad accounts. Real money, real clients, real consequences. Would an agency hand all of that to a generic connector with no guardrails? I don't think many would. One poorly constrained prompt can burn through a client's monthly budget in hours. That's not theoretical.

Spend caps, bid strategy validation, pacing limits, anomaly detection. These features exist because people got burned, and because the team building the product has spent enough time in the domain to know what goes wrong. A generic connector gives you *access*. A specialist product gives you *judgement*. I touched on a related idea in [The Accountability Gap](/blog/the-accountability-gap): people don't just pay for a service, they pay for someone else to have thought deeply about the problem. That applies to software too.

## Guardrails as encoded experience

The real value of specialist software isn't the API connection. It's the thousands of edge cases the team has already encountered and handled. Compliance requirements in regulated domains where "it usually works" isn't sufficient. Understanding what the AI should *never* do, which often matters more than what it can do. The accumulated scar tissue from years in a specific vertical that a generalist tool simply doesn't have.

## Meeting people where they are

I've been [thinking about this for a while](/blog/stop-slapping-chat-on-everything). The best AI interface is often the one people already use. Not another dashboard, not another login. WhatsApp, Slack, email. Tools with billions of hours of muscle memory behind them.

I think OpenClaw's most important contribution is less about replacing SaaS and more about shifting expectations. It's demonstrated that AI should come to you, rather than requiring you to go to it. That's a really valuable insight for the whole ecosystem.

The interesting opportunity for AI SaaS products is to participate in this. Expose your specialist layer via MCP. Let your guardrails, your domain knowledge, your compliance be accessible through the protocol. You get the reach of the open ecosystem, users get the safety of a purpose-built product.

## The bar goes up

If your product is essentially "AI plus an API connector", then yes, OpenClaw probably does make you redundant. That kind of product was always one good plugin away from irrelevance.

But if your product encodes genuine domain expertise, real guardrails, real accountability, then OpenClaw looks more like a distribution channel than a competitor.

Realistically, a lot of people will gravitate the other way. It's never been easier to ship something shallow. But that's exactly the point: if one person with a Claude Code subscription can blast your product out of the market in a weekend, you didn't have a moat. You had a head start, and head starts don't last.

If you want to build something that endures, something with real impact, you have to go deep. Domain expertise, hard-won guardrails, genuine accountability. Those are the things that can't be replicated by a generic tool and a good prompt. That's where the moat is now.
