Last time I wrote about [whether we still need experts](/blog/do-we-still-need-experts), my answer was yes. I gave the reasoning I had: broad vocabulary, lived experience, knowing what to put inside a VPC. All true, all anecdotal. I wanted to come back with the actual argument.

Here is the version with maths.

The shorter version, if you stop reading at the next sentence: you cannot compensate for lack of information with more agents. Not because the agents are bad. Because of the data processing inequality.

## The cone

Picture a starting point. From it, the agent makes a choice, then another, then another. Each choice has some distribution over alternatives. Plot all of those branchings forward and you get a cone, narrow at the start, fanning out over time.

The cone has a name in information theory. The whole sequence of actions an agent might take is a trajectory $\tau = (a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_T)$ (read $\tau$ aloud as "tau"). The total uncertainty over that whole sequence is its entropy $H(\tau)$, read aloud as "the entropy of tau." That single number is the cone, expressed as one quantity.

You can think of the agent as picking each next action based on what has happened so far. State $s_t$ at step $t$, action $a_t$ chosen, world advances, state $s_{t+1}$, repeat. That style of policy is called *autoregressive* (each step conditions on the past), and it lets the total uncertainty split cleanly into a sum of per-step uncertainties:

$$
H(\tau) = \sum_t H(a_t \mid s_t)
$$

In plain English: each step contributes its own dose of uncertainty to the cone, and the long-horizon uncertainty is just the sum of all those doses. That is why long tasks compound. Each fresh action gets to add fresh uncertainty on top of what was already accumulated.

The cone grows monotonically with horizon. Long task, wide cone. This is what makes "build me a todo app" different from "build me an AI SaaS with four billing tiers, an MCP, and Google Ads integration." Both start at the same point. One ends in a wide grey region of possible futures.

## Steering is mutual information

Now overlay the operator. Behind the operator there is a hidden thing $Y$, what they actually want. They cannot show you $Y$ directly. They can only write a prompt $P$, which is a compressed sketch of $Y$ squeezed through their fingers.

The question is how much of $Y$ survives the journey. Information theory gives that a name too: *mutual information*, written $I(Y; \tau)$ and read aloud as "the mutual information of Y and tau." It measures how much of one quantity you can read off from another. If $I(Y; \tau)$ is high, watching the agent's trajectory lets you reconstruct what the operator wanted. If it is zero, the agent's output tells you nothing about intent. It could be anything.

Precise prompting raises mutual information. Technical vocabulary raises it. Lived experience raises it, because lived experience is a denser encoder: you choose the few words that compress the most bits. A senior engineer says "use a VPC with public and private subnets" and that is twenty bits of architectural intent in eight words. A junior either does not know to say it, or spends two paragraphs saying it less precisely.

This is what I was reaching for in the last post when I said generalist vocabulary helps. The formal version: experience is bandwidth.

Jeon, Lee, Lei and Van Roy did the [careful version of this argument](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.15530) at ICML 2024. Error decays at a rate governed by $I(\text{prompt}; \text{task})$. Diminishing returns per additional bit. Each new word in the prompt buys you less than the last.

## Specification has a price

Rate-distortion theory gives the price. To keep expected deviation below some tolerance $\varepsilon$, you need $R(\varepsilon)$ bits of specification. $R$ is not linear in task complexity. Small $\varepsilon$, complex task, and $R$ blows up.

This is the lived experience of every engineer who has tried to prompt their way to a complete feature. You say more, you say it more carefully, you add the corner cases, and you can feel $R$ climbing faster than your patience.

The context window is finite. Call its capacity $C$. If $R(\varepsilon) > C$, no amount of cleverness will close the cone below $\varepsilon$. You hit the wall.

You start writing the prompt that fully specifies the enterprise-tier billing logic and you realise you have consumed eight thousand tokens and the agent hasn't done anything yet. You compress, you abbreviate, you let some things slide, and now you are below $R(\varepsilon)$ and the cone is going to widen on those exact corners. This is unavoidable. You cannot specify your way out of a complex task within a finite context.

## You cannot fix this with more agents

Which brings me to the section I actually wanted to write.

The instinct, when one agent struggles, is to throw more agents at it. Swarms. Teams. Multi-agent frameworks. Orchestrator plus worker plus critic plus reviewer plus planner. The premise is that parallelism scales capability.

It does. But only in one direction, and not the one that matters when the bottleneck is intent.

The data processing inequality is the formal version of the rule that you cannot get information out of a system that was never put in. Take any function $f$ you like, anything you do to the prompt: a single agent, a chain of agents, an orchestrator routing tasks, a voting scheme over a swarm, even a wildly clever pipeline you have not invented yet. Call its output $f(P)$. Then:

$$
I(f(P); Y) \leq I(P; Y)
$$

In English: whatever you do to the prompt after it has been written, the result cannot share *more* information with operator intent $Y$ than the prompt itself does. Downstream processing can rearrange the bits, lose some, present them better, but it cannot synthesise bits about intent that were never there. You cannot compute your way out of an underspecified objective.

There are three things more agents *can* do, and they are worth naming carefully because they look like exceptions but are not:

1. **Variance reduction.** Multiple samples from the same conditional distribution. Useful when your bottleneck is stochasticity around a correct mean. Not useful when you are biased toward the wrong attractor in the first place.
2. **Genuine new observations.** If each agent goes off and reads a doc, runs a probe, queries a database, executes the code, they pull in new information from outside the original prompt. That adds bits. But it is the act of querying the world that adds them, not the parallelism.
3. **Active queries to the operator.** [Sadigh et al. (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04303) showed agents can extract more reward signal by asking humans maximally-informative comparisons. Again, the bits come from the operator's answers, not the parallelism.

What more agents fundamentally cannot do:

- Recover intent that was never in the prompt.
- Distinguish between competent pursuit of the wrong proxy and competent pursuit of the right one. That is [goal misgeneralisation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.14111), and it is an empirical fact about every long-running agent system I have seen.
- Reduce bias below the mutual-information floor set by your spec.

There is a tempting Condorcet-jury rebuttal: the wisdom of crowds, surely $n$ agents converging is better than one. The Condorcet result requires *independent* errors. Agents drawn from the same base model and given the same prompt do not have independent errors. They have heavily correlated errors. You are not building a juror panel; you are sampling one juror $n$ times. The variance shrinks. The bias does not move.

If your swarm of agents is producing impressive-looking work that keeps subtly diverging from what you actually wanted, the bottleneck is not throughput. The bottleneck is the bits of intent you have transmitted into the system. Adding more agents is, mathematically, the wrong intervention.

## Memory doesn't close it either

The natural follow-up is: surely agentic memory closes the gap? An agent that remembers every past interaction has all the bits of intent the operator has ever produced.

It doesn't help. Memory captures *past* intent. The cone problem stems partly from intent being non-stationary: humans don't know what they want until they see it. [Inverse Reward Design](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.02827) makes this formal: the spec the operator writes is *evidence about* their latent reward, not the reward itself. [Cooperative IRL](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.03137) treats the whole loop as a game where the agent continuously infers an evolving reward.

Even an oracle with infinite memory cannot close the cone, because the operator-side distribution itself is moving. The iterative loop (prompt, observe, correct, prompt again) is not a workaround for primitive tools. It is the only way to add bits at the rate at which intent resolves.

## What we actually see

If this argument is right, we should observe reliability decaying with horizon even as raw capability rises. Compounding error in long chains. Goal misgeneralisation on underspecified tasks. All of which we do see.

METR's [task horizon work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14499) (Kwa, West et al. 2025) measured a doubling every roughly seven months in the length of task a frontier model can complete with 50% success. Worth celebrating. Except they also measured that the *80%-reliability horizon is roughly five times shorter than the 50% horizon for the same model*. Reliability collapses long before capability does. The cone matters more than the mean.

Dziri et al.'s [Faith and Fate](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18654) found roughly exponential accuracy decay with reasoning depth on compositional tasks: four-digit multiplication near zero accuracy while two-digit is near one hundred. DeepMind's [specification gaming catalogue](https://deepmind.google/discover/blog/specification-gaming-the-flip-side-of-ai-ingenuity/) has documented something like ninety cases of agents satisfying the literal spec while violating intent.

This is the empirical signature of a cone that widens faster than bits can be supplied.

## The controller view

What is the expert actually doing? Acting as a controller on a stochastic process. [LLMPC (Maher 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02486) makes the analogy explicit: LLM planning as model-predictive control. The operator observes the emerging trajectory, identifies divergence early, and injects high-bandwidth corrections at the moments where they compress the most bits-per-token. [Korbak, Perez and Buckley (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.11275) showed that KL-regularised RL is variational Bayesian inference: the base model is a prior, every reward signal is evidence updating it. The operator at the wheel is doing the same thing in real time.

This is why a few well-timed words from a senior engineer outperform a swarm. Not because the swarm is incompetent. Because the senior is injecting bits at exactly the points where the agent is about to fork into the wrong cone.

Experience is bandwidth, and timing is amplification.

## So, do we still need experts?

Last time I argued yes from vibes. The maths gives the same answer.

You cannot compensate for lack of information with more agents. Not because the agents are bad. Because the data processing inequality is what it is. Throwing more compute downstream of an impoverished prompt does not synthesise the bits you never sent.

If your team feels like it is spinning up more agents and getting subtly worse outcomes, that is not a tooling problem. That is the data processing inequality, billing you for the bits you never sent.
