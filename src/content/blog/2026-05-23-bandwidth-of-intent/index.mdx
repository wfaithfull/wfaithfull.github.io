Last time I wrote about [whether we still need experts](/blog/do-we-still-need-experts), my answer was yes. Thinking about it since, I found myself returning to ideas from my PhD: entropy, mutual information, the data processing inequality. Could those underpin the intuition with something firmer?

> *N.B. I'm not pretending to still be a researcher, nor that I've kept a deep grasp of information theory - I did in a past life. I've also used Opus 4.7 to help compile the idea into a grounded argument. The nice thing about publishing on a blog is that none of this has to pass peer review.*

The argument: agents are bounded by how much intent we can transmit into them. On long horizons that bound is tight, and agents almost certainly drift from human intent without ongoing steering.

## The cone

Picture a starting point. From it, the agent makes a choice, then another, then another. Each choice has some distribution over alternatives. Plot all those branchings forward and you get a cone, narrow at the start, fanning out over time.

The cone has a formal version. Call the whole sequence of an agent's actions a trajectory $\tau = (a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_T)$ (read $\tau$ aloud as "tau"). Its total uncertainty is the entropy $H(\tau)$ ("the entropy of tau"). That single number *is* the cone. If the agent picks each action conditioned on what came before (an *autoregressive* policy), the chain rule splits the total entropy into a sum of per-step uncertainties:

$$
H(\tau) = \sum_t H(a_t \mid s_t)
$$

Each step adds its own dose of uncertainty on top of what's already accumulated, which is why long tasks compound. (Strictly, $s_t$ here is shorthand for the full running context, not a compressed Markov state - that's the safer reading for an LLM agent.)

The cone grows monotonically with horizon. Long task, wide cone. This is what makes "build me a todo app" different from "build me a multi-tenant SaaS with single sign-on, role-based access, audit logging, and a billing system." Even the second prompt leaves huge questions open: what are the pricing tiers, who can see whose data, what does the audit log record and for how long? Every unspecified corner is another direction the cone gets to fan into.

The cone isn't uniform, though. On familiar territory, a well-trained policy has near-zero conditional entropy per step - it's seen the pattern a thousand times and barely needs to choose. In that regime the cone is really a narrow tube hugging the data manifold; it fans wildly only at *novelty boundaries* where the model hasn't seen the pattern, or where several plausible continuations exist and nothing in the prompt picks between them. Boilerplate is a tube. Genuinely new work is a cone.

## Steering is mutual information

Overlay the operator. They have some hidden $Y$, what they actually want. They can't show $Y$ directly; they can only write a prompt $P$, a compressed sketch of $Y$ squeezed through their fingers.

How much of $Y$ survives the journey is what information theory calls *mutual information*, $I(Y; \tau)$ ("the mutual information of Y and tau"). High means you can reconstruct intent from the trajectory; zero means the output tells you nothing about it. Precise prompting, technical vocabulary, and lived experience all raise it - experience because it's a denser encoder, picking the few words that compress the most bits. A senior says "use a VPC with public and private subnets" and packs significant architectural intent into eight words; a junior either doesn't know to say it or spends two paragraphs saying it less precisely.

Jeon, Lee, Lei and Van Roy did the [careful version of this](https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.15530) at ICML 2024: error decays at a rate governed by $I(\text{prompt}; \text{task})$, with diminishing returns per additional bit.

## The prior does some of the work for free

The agent isn't a blank slate. It's been trained on an enormous corpus of human-produced text, itself a sample from the distribution of human intent, so some of $Y$ is already encoded into the weights before the prompt is written.

Call those weights $\theta$ ("theta"). The intent-information now splits into two reservoirs:

$$
I(f(P, \theta); Y) \leq I(P; Y) + I(\theta; Y \mid P)
$$

"The mutual information of f-of-P-and-theta with Y is at most the mutual information of P and Y, plus the mutual information of theta and Y given P." The `|` reads "given" and means "after accounting for what the prompt already told us."

The model knows things about what humans usually want. "Build me a todo app" is a tiny prompt riding on a much bigger prior - the agent has seen thousands of todo apps and already knows the modal architecture. This is why instruction-tuned models feel surprisingly helpful on common tasks: fitting is explicitly trying to maximise the alignment between $\theta$ and population-average intent.

It cuts the other way too. The prior helps in inverse proportion to how unusual your intent is. Close to the population mean, the weights carry most of the load; in the tail (the taste, the repo-specific idiosyncrasies, the unwritten constraints your team learned the hard way) the prior gives you nothing, and the bits have to come from the prompt. That's where experience pays off: not at expressing population-average intent, but at transmitting the tail intent the prior can't supply.

## What about synthesis at intersections?

A fair pushback: the prior isn't just modal completion. It's a compositional generative model, and LLMs can produce genuinely novel work at the intersection of concepts they've seen. GPT-5.5 cracking an open Erdős problem makes "the prior just gives you the modal answer" sound badly understated.

The tail intent argument survives this, but you have to notice what's actually present in those wins: a crystal-clear target (the conjecture statement is dense intent), a tight verifier (the proof checker says yes or no), and purely behavioural intent (nobody cares what the proof looks like, only whether it checks).

So the honest version: capability synthesis substitutes for human-in-loop bits *only when there's a tight verifier*. Without one, synthesis produces more confident-looking candidates with no way to pick between them. That's why frontier wins cluster in formal-verifier domains (theorem proving, competitive programming, optimisation) and move slower where "works" has a thousand valid solutions and operator taste does the selecting.

## Specification has a price

Rate-distortion theory gives the price. To keep expected deviation below tolerance $\varepsilon$ ("epsilon"), you need $R(\varepsilon)$ bits of specification, and $R$ isn't linear: small $\varepsilon$, complex task, $R$ blows up. The context window is finite; if $R(\varepsilon)$ exceeds it, no cleverness closes the cone. Every engineer who's tried to prompt a complete feature has felt this - you say more, you add the corner cases, eventually you compress and abbreviate, and the cone widens on those exact corners.

The bit-count framing also misses something: not every bit is equally useful. A prompt that pins down the high-leverage decisions (architecture, data model, constraints that ripple downstream) is worth far more than one with the same bit count spent on variable naming. Bit *placement* matters more than bit count. Over-specification cuts the other way too, pulling the model off the manifold its prior is good at, into worse outputs than the modal answer would have been. There's a Goldilocks zone, and finding it is its own skill.

## You cannot fix this with more agents

The instinct when one agent struggles is to throw more at it: swarms, teams, orchestrator-plus-worker-plus-critic. Parallelism scales capability, but not the dimension that matters when intent is the bottleneck.

The data processing inequality says you can't get information out of a system that was never put in. For any pipeline $f$ (a chain of agents, a voting scheme, anything you build downstream of the prompt):

$$
I(f(P); Y) \leq I(P; Y)
$$

The output can't share more information with operator intent $Y$ than the prompt did. Processing rearranges and loses bits; it cannot synthesise bits that were never there.

The DPI assumes a *Markov chain* (read "Mark-off") $Y \to P \to f(P)$: intent into prompt, prompt into output, no side channels. If $f$ has its own line to $Y$ (through training, through tool calls, through asking the operator), the chain breaks and the bound relaxes. So DPI bounds the *novel* bits a pipeline can extract beyond what the prior supplies and what the environment can be probed for. That leaves three things more agents can genuinely do:

1. **Variance reduction around a well-placed prior.** If the posterior over $Y$ is concentrated on the right mode but noisy, sampling and voting helps. The trap: if the prior is on the wrong mode, more samples just consolidate the wrong answer with more confidence.
2. **Genuine new observations.** Reading a doc, running a probe, executing the code - bits flow in from the world, not from parallelism.
3. **Active queries to the operator.** [Sadigh et al. (2017)](https://arxiv.org/abs/1810.04303) showed agents can extract more reward signal by asking humans maximally-informative comparisons. Again, the bits come from the operator, not the parallelism.

What they cannot do is recover intent that was never in the prompt, distinguish competent pursuit of the wrong proxy from the right one ([goal misgeneralisation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2105.14111)), or reduce bias below the mutual-information floor your spec sets.

The Condorcet-jury rebuttal - surely $n$ agents converging beats one - requires *independent* errors. Agents from the same base model on the same prompt have heavily correlated errors. Different personas, different base models, or adversarial critics partially decorrelate them, but you're still sampling from a small region of policy space, still working below the intent ceiling. Variance shrinks; bias barely moves.

## Memory helps, but only with the stationary part

Doesn't agentic memory close the gap? It helps, but partially. The *stationary* part of intent (coding style, repo conventions, the architectural decisions you've already made, the team's accumulated preferences) gets captured and persists across sessions - the slow accumulation of a personal prior, the same prior a senior colleague builds up by being around for a couple of years. What memory can't capture is the part of intent that hasn't happened yet. Humans don't fully know what they want until they see a candidate. [Inverse Reward Design](https://arxiv.org/abs/1711.02827) makes this formal: the spec is *evidence about* the operator's latent reward, not the reward itself. [Cooperative IRL](https://arxiv.org/abs/1606.03137) treats the loop as a game where the agent continuously infers a drifting reward.

Memory shores up the stationary part; the iterative loop (prompt, observe, correct, prompt again) handles the non-stationary residue. Neither replaces the other.

## What we actually see

If the argument is right, reliability should decay with horizon even as raw capability rises, with goal misgeneralisation showing up on underspecified tasks. We do see this. METR's [task horizon work](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14499) (Kwa, West et al. 2025) measured a doubling every roughly seven months in the length of task a frontier model can complete with 50% success - but also that the 80%-reliability horizon is roughly five times shorter than the 50% horizon for the same model. Reliability collapses long before capability does. Dziri et al.'s [Faith and Fate](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.18654) showed roughly exponential accuracy decay with reasoning depth on compositional tasks; DeepMind's [specification gaming catalogue](https://deepmind.google/discover/blog/specification-gaming-the-flip-side-of-ai-ingenuity/) lists around ninety cases of agents satisfying the literal spec while violating intent. The empirical signature of a cone widening faster than bits can be supplied.

## The controller view

The expert is a controller on a stochastic process. [LLMPC (Maher 2025)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02486) makes the analogy explicit: LLM planning as model-predictive control. The operator watches the trajectory, identifies divergence early, and injects high-bandwidth corrections at the moments where they compress the most bits-per-token. [Korbak, Perez and Buckley (2022)](https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.11275) framed KL-regularised RL as variational Bayesian inference: the base model is a prior, every reward signal updates it; the operator at the wheel does the same in real time. A few well-timed words from a senior outperform a swarm because the senior is injecting bits at the points where the agent is about to fork into the wrong cone.

Experience is bandwidth. Timing is amplification.

## Two flavours of intent

Split $Y$ into two pieces; the argument applies very differently to each. $Y_\text{behavioural}$ ("Y-behavioural") is what the system should *do* - externally verifiable, testable, the acceptance criteria. $Y_\text{aesthetic}$ ("Y-aesthetic") is *how* it should do it: which abstractions feel right, when to add a flag versus refactor, whether this is the moment for a generic helper or three almost-identical copies. The thousand small choices that compound into "this codebase has soul" or "this codebase reads like it was generated on a Tuesday".

$Y_\text{behavioural}$ compresses cheaply - write the tests and the bits flow in through a channel that isn't the prose prompt, with the test suite acting as a constant correction signal. $Y_\text{aesthetic}$ has no easy compression. You can't write a test for "this should look like Will wrote it". Those bits come from in-band steering, from a human who's seen enough of your work to encode the taste in a sentence at the right moment.

Expert steering matters most where intent resists testification. Specify in tests where you can; save the prose bandwidth for the taste.

## So, do we still need experts?

Last time I argued yes from vibes. The maths gives the same answer with a bit more shape.

The prior gets you the modal answer for free; the tail is where experts earn their keep, and the only channel for tail intent is the prompt, tests, tool returns, or a human at the wheel correcting in real time. Adding more agents doesn't change that - the DPI is the DPI. The practical version of the claim is narrower than the mathematical one, though. It bites hardest when intent really is the binding constraint, and current agentic systems often lose bits to other things first: hallucination, planning failures, attention drift, bad orchestration. It also assumes the model could do the task if it knew what you wanted; for work beyond current capability, more bits won't help. What's left is the regime where the model could do the task but is picking wrong from many valid candidates. That's most professional software engineering.

A few directions this could turn out wrong. If execution improves vastly while specification stays the same, intent bandwidth was never the binding constraint. If elicitation interfaces compress more intent into fewer tokens, the expert zone shrinks. RLHF keeps folding operator preferences back into $\theta$, so today's tail becomes tomorrow's modal. The argument survives all three; those are just the places I'd watch.

If your team feels like it's spinning up more agents and getting subtly worse outcomes, sometimes that's not a tooling problem. Sometimes it's the data processing inequality, billing you for the bits you never sent.
